What a data product contains

Seven clauses, one shape — every governed data product carries this template. Shown in four states: today's informal baseline, a target template, and a concrete Session example. Aligned with the Open Data Product Standard v1.0.0 ↗.

What a data product contains — four states
State 1 · Today template
Informal data — today's baseline
No formal spec. Data exists as a byproduct: a table, an export, owned implicitly by whoever built it. Schema, contracts, quality, and governance live in tribal knowledge — if at all. Most Laerdal data starts here.
The baseline before the governed model is applied. Documenting it makes the gap to the target visible.
Today template pending — what "no governance" looks like in practice will be captured once we have a representative real-world asset to map from.

A real "today" example is being identified — most likely an existing product data asset. This state will make the gap between today and the target concrete and discussable.
State 3 · Target template
Full data product — template
The 7-clause shape every governed data product carries. Owned by the Domain, consumed by named groups. The same template applies to both source-aligned products (one producer, one pipeline) and aggregated products (multiple producers joined on a shared key) — the Composition section is where they diverge.
Same template whether the data product feeds dashboards, AI agents, or analytical workflows.

Data product classification

Every data product is classified by how consumers expect to use it. The Session data product is an Enabler. Separate from classification, each product also has a product type (source-aligned, aggregated, etc.) that describes how it is built — see §1 Identity.

Classification Use case Market Success measures
Utility When a consumer expects a product to be instantly available, for all purposes Internal or external Product awareness, availability and time to access
Enabler When a consumer expects a product to enable decision making for specific business outcomes Internal or external Time or money saved in the process of achieving a specific business outcome
Driver When the product is expected to drive the business Typically focused on external monetisation by selling for economic value Revenue generated
