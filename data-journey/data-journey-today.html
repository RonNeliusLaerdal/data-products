The pipeline may run and the data may land in the catalog. But without a contract, a schema, or a named owner, no consumer can know what the data means — or whether to trust it.
What's missing today

Start with the root problem: most data is never really shared — it stays inside the product that created it. Everything below makes that harder to fix.

No clear owner
No one is accountable. When consumers have questions, there is no one to ask.
Hard to find
Data exists but isn't described or documented as a product. Every consumer figures out what it means from scratch.
No contract
Nothing states what the data contains, how often it updates, or what to expect. Consumers cannot rely on it.
Access by request
Data access is tightly controlled. Getting it requires manual requests and approvals — before analysis can even begin.
No schema
Field names, types, and meanings are not formally defined. Consumers reverse-engineer what they can from code.
Why it matters

Data always gets reused.

Today's data will be picked up by a new analyst, another product, or an AI agent. If its meaning and owner were never captured, they can't understand it — and there's no one to ask.

AI needs clear data.

People can work around ambiguity. AI can't — it takes data at face value, so missing meaning becomes a confident wrong answer, at scale.Gartner, Data Management Challenges of Model Context Protocol, 04.2026

What it costs us

Things break quietly.

When a producer changes something, no one is told. Dashboards break, and the first sign is someone noticing the numbers are wrong. When those numbers are customer-facing, trust goes with them.

Things never get built.

The bigger loss is quieter: the insights and AI products that never happen because no one offered the data as a product.

The shift

Data is a product, not a byproduct.

Every gap above has the same fix: data with an owner, a contract, a schema, and a quality bar — offered on purpose.
