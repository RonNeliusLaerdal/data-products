Scenario Data as a Laerdal Data Product

A proposal for publishing Scenario Editor content through Unity Catalog — so AI and analytics teams can access it through one shared, managed path, rather than each building their own direct integration.

The proposal in one sentence We are not changing how simulators consume scenarios today. We are asking whether Scenario Editor can also publish a governed, downstream projection of scenario data — one that makes the same content accessible to AI and analytics in a safer, more scalable, and more manageable way.
Context

Scenarios are becoming a shared reference point

Scenario Editor is already where simulation content lives. Scenarios sit at the center of how simulation is designed, how it runs, how AI can support it, and what we can eventually learn from it. The question is whether that content can also be made accessible in a structured, governed way — not just for simulators, but for the teams building on top of simulation.

Today's reality

Every team that needs scenario context builds its own direct connection into Scenario Editor's backend. Each integration is undocumented, couples the consumer to internal implementation, and must be rebuilt from scratch by the next team that needs it.

What we're proposing

Scenario Editor publishes a governed, read-only catalog of scenario data through Unity Catalog. One managed path. Any approved AI or analytics team can discover it, understand it, and build on it — without reverse-engineering the source.

Why we need it

Three use cases drive this

Each use case is independent, but they share the same foundation: a stable, accessible layer of scenario data in the catalog.

1
Near-term

Samaritan Companion

Samaritan is being built as a context-aware assistant that connects data across Laerdal's products. For simulation, it needs to know what scenario was running — the patient, the clinical setting, the narrative — to interpret what happened during a session. Without that context, it can see what a learner did, but not what the situation called for.

Example A learner finishes a simulation and asks Samaritan "did I do the right thing?" Samaritan can see every action the learner took — but not what the scenario was asking them to do. Without that, it is like a teacher marking a test without the questions. It can describe what happened, but not whether it was right or wrong for this situation.

Publishing through Unity Catalog is not the only way to give Samaritan that context — but it is the better way. Any team building on scenario data finds it through the catalog, not through inside knowledge of Scenario Editor's backend. It carries its own schema and contract, so each new consumer does not have to reverse-engineer the source. Every direct integration, by contrast, is an undocumented dependency that couples consumers to Scenario Editor's internal implementation.

2
Near-term

Portfolio visibility for PSim

Scenario Editor is positioned as the content hub for simulation across the Laerdal ecosystem. But right now there is no way to see how that content is actually being used. How many scenarios are actively running across SimGo deployments? Which are being reused across customers? Which are sitting dormant? Where are the gaps?

Example Right now, scenarios leave Scenario Editor and become invisible. PSim has no way of knowing whether a scenario is being run daily across dozens of customer sites, or has never been opened once. A catalog view would change that — and give PSim direct line of sight into the real-world reach of what they build.

A governed catalog view creates that picture, and gives PSim visible evidence of Scenario Editor's reach and impact across the portfolio.

3
Longer term

Design intelligence

Once scenario definitions can be joined with session outcomes, we can start answering questions that are not currently possible. If scenarios that teams keep coming back to share something in common — a certain structure, a length, a type of situation — that is a signal worth knowing. It should change how new scenarios are designed.

Example This feedback loop does not currently exist. Connecting scenario design data with session outcomes would create it, and the answers feed directly back into how scenarios are built. The scenario ID becomes the natural link between the authored scenario and the outcome.

This is not the first step — but it is a strong reason to build the foundation properly now, rather than having to retrofit it later.

What we are proposing

Two flows, kept separate

The operational flow already exists and we are not touching it. The data product flow is additive — a separate, read-only projection for AI and analytics use.

Architecture overview
Operational flow — unchanged

How simulators consume scenarios

Scenario Editor packages scenarios. SimGo and other simulators consume them to run simulations. This stays as it is — fast, direct, reliable. We are not moving simulation execution into a data platform.

Scenario Editor → SimGo → Session runs
No changes to this path.
Data product flow — additive

How AI and analytics access scenarios

Scenario Editor publishes a curated scenario catalog to Unity Catalog: a read-only projection of the scenario library. Not the same artifact simulators consume. Its purpose is to expose a stable, useful layer of scenario information through one managed path.

Scenario Editor → Unity Catalog → AI · Analytics
The scenario ID links authored scenarios with session outcomes.
How we get there

Schema comes first

Before we can publish anything, we need a shared understanding of scenario structure as it exists today. The schema session already on the calendar is exactly designed to do this.

Why this matters beyond the data product

If the scenario structure is mostly understood implicitly in the codebase rather than explicitly documented, that creates risk in every existing dependency — not just a future pipeline. The same structural ambiguity that could break a downstream data consumer could also cause friction in the operational connection between Scenario Editor and the products that consume it today.

Mapping the schema is valuable regardless of whether the data product moves ahead immediately. It clarifies the existing contract surface for everyone who depends on it.

What we'd like to do together

One shared session, one concrete output

The goal is not to finalize a contract. The goal is to build a shared foundation — which aligns with the schema session already on the calendar.

Walk through the current scenario structure together — what fields and objects exist today. From there, identify what a realistic first publishable version could look like, and surface any concerns on the PSim side: data sensitivity, customer boundaries, product flexibility, or structures that should stay outside the catalog.

From there, the Data and AI team turns the output into a concrete proposal covering ingestion, governance, and contract design. That proposal comes back to PSim before anything moves.

The point is not to finalize a contract in one meeting. The point is to build a shared foundation — and that is exactly what the schema session is already designed to do.

In short

What we are asking — and what we are not

✕
We are not asking you to redesign the operational flow or change how simulators consume scenarios.
✕
We are not asking PSim to give up flexibility in Scenario Editor's internal model.
✕

✓
